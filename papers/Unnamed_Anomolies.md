# 📘 Unnamed Anomalies:  
**A White Paper on Structural Exploitation Without Legal or Social Classification**

---

## 📄 Executive Summary

Throughout modern systems, corporate, governmental, and technological, there exist recurring patterns of behavior that systematically exploit human cognition, trust, and time. These patterns are not illegal. They are not classified as misconduct. They are not named.

This paper identifies and classifies these *unnamed anomalies*, structural behaviors that benefit specific actors while imposing hidden costs on individuals and society. By naming them, we aim to make them visible, and by making them visible, we make them subject to accountability.

---

## 1. Introduction

In every large-scale system, there are behaviors that are:

- **Widely practiced**
- **Systematically beneficial to certain actors**
- **Recognized as problematic by those affected**
- **Not defined in any legal or ethical framework**

These behaviors exist in plain sight. They are not hidden. They are simply *unnamed*. Because they have no name, they cannot be easily discussed, challenged, or corrected. They operate through the absence of language, not through secrecy.

This paper seeks to give these patterns their first formal classification.

## 2. Classification of Unnamed Anomalies

### 2.1 Intentional Ignorance (II)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | The deliberate choice not to acquire or acknowledge information that would require action or accountability. |
| **Mechanism** | Creating barriers to information, delegating responsibility, or maintaining plausible deniability. |
| **Beneficiary** | Leadership, executives, and institutions that avoid liability. |
| **Cost** | Increased risk to individuals, systemic decay, and delayed accountability. |

**Example:** A company chooses not to audit its AI systems, knowing that the audit would reveal structural harm. By not knowing, they avoid responsibility.

---

### 2.2 Systemic Delay (SD)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | The strategic prolongation of processes to exhaust opponents, reduce public attention, or allow actors to benefit from time. |
| **Mechanism** | Expanding timelines, citing bureaucratic requirements, requiring multiple rounds of review. |
| **Beneficiary** | Well-resourced actors, regulatory bodies, and incumbents who benefit from the status quo. |
| **Cost** | Exhaustion of those seeking accountability, increased financial burden, erosion of trust. |

**Example:** A regulatory body delays action on a known AI risk by repeatedly requesting additional documentation, effectively stalling any meaningful intervention.

---

### 2.3 Complexity as Weapon (CaaW)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | The use of unnecessary complexity to obscure meaning, avoid accountability, or prevent participation. |
| **Mechanism** | Technical jargon, convoluted explanations, citing "systemic complexity." |
| **Beneficiary** | Technical experts, large organizations, and those with resources to interpret complexity. |
| **Cost** | Exclusion of non-experts, reduced transparency, increased reliance on intermediaries. |

**Example:** A company explains harmful AI behavior as "an unintended outcome of complex neural architectures," making it impossible for outsiders to challenge.

---

### 2.4 Obligation Shifting (OS)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | The transfer of responsibility, risk, or burden from one actor to another without acknowledgment. |
| **Mechanism** | Terms of service, disclaimers, contracts, and public statements. |
| **Beneficiary** | Large entities that can absorb legal costs, while individuals bear the practical burden. |
| **Cost** | Disproportionate burden on individuals, reduced accountability, erosion of trust. |

**Example:** A platform adds a clause to its terms of service that shifts all liability for AI-generated content to the user, without notifying them of the change.

---

### 2.5 Neutrality as Shield (NaS)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | The use of neutrality claims to avoid taking sides, while actions consistently benefit one side. |
| **Mechanism** | Declaring neutrality, refusing to take positions, citing "fairness." |
| **Beneficiary** | Those who benefit from the status quo, often incumbents and powerful actors. |
| **Cost** | Disproportionate harm to those not benefiting, reduced trust in neutral institutions. |

**Example:** A regulatory agency claims neutrality while consistently acting in ways that favor large AI providers over smaller competitors.

---

### 2.6 Procedural Abyss (PA)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | The creation of endless processes that never lead to a decision or outcome. |
| **Mechanism** | Mandating reviews, studies, consultations, or meetings without a clear endpoint. |
| **Beneficiary** | Those who benefit from the absence of a decision, often incumbents. |
| **Cost** | Exhaustion, cynicism, and belief that the system cannot change. |

**Example:** An organization commits to "studying" the impact of its AI systems but never completes the study, avoiding any obligation to act.

## 3. The Common Structure of Unnamed Anomalies

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Visibility** | The behavior is visible to those affected but lacks public or legal recognition. |
| **Absence of Language** | There is no standard term for the behavior, making it hard to discuss. |
| **Systemic Benefit** | The behavior consistently benefits powerful actors. |
| **Hidden Cost** | The cost is borne by those with less power, awareness, or recourse. |
| **Normalization** | The behavior is so common that it is treated as normal. |

## 7. Additional Anomalies 

### 7.1 Manufactured Urgency (MU)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | The artificial creation of time pressure to force decisions without adequate deliberation. |
| **Mechanism** | Deadlines, limited-time offers, "act now" framing, or sudden changes in policy timelines. |
| **Beneficiary** | Those who benefit from hasty decisions, often sellers, platforms, or policy-makers. |
| **Cost** | Poor decisions, regret, manipulation of cognitive bandwidth. |
| **Example** | A software update with critical security patches and an "opt-out within 48 hours" clause, forcing users to accept new terms without reading them. |

---

### 7.2 Output Overload (OO)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | The production of excessive, low-quality, or irrelevant information to obscure actionable data. |
| **Mechanism** | Over-reporting, jargon-heavy documentation, irrelevant metrics. |
| **Beneficiary** | Those who benefit from confusion or information paralysis. |
| **Cost** | Cognitive exhaustion, delayed decisions, missed signals. |
| **Example** | An AI company publishes 100-page technical reports filled with charts that reveal no actual insight about model behavior. |

---

### 7.3 Structural Amnesia (SA)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | The systematic forgetting of past incidents, failures, or commitments to avoid accountability. |
| **Mechanism** | No documentation, no follow-up, high turnover, or shifting blame. |
| **Beneficiary** | Organizations that wish to avoid legal or reputational consequences. |
| **Cost** | Repetition of mistakes, erosion of institutional memory, loss of trust. |
| **Example** | A company whose AI caused harm in 2024 produces a new model in 2026 with no mention of past issues, claiming “lessons learned” with no evidence. |

---

### 7.4 The Rhetorical Loop (RL)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | Circular communication that appears to respond but advances no information. |
| **Mechanism** | Repetition, paraphrasing, restating the question as the answer. |
| **Beneficiary** | Those who wish to appear responsive without actually responding. |
| **Cost** | Wasted time, frustration, erosion of trust. |
| **Example** | A regulator repeatedly says “We are reviewing the matter” for months, with no new information ever provided. |

---

### 7.5 Accountability Diffusion (AD)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | The distribution of responsibility across so many actors that no single actor can be held accountable. |
| **Mechanism** | Multiple departments, subcontractors, joint ventures, or vague role definitions. |
| **Beneficiary** | Large organizations, governments, and consortia. |
| **Cost** | Impunity, lack of redress, systemic abuse. |
| **Example** | An AI system causes harm, but the provider blames the cloud host, the cloud host blames the chipmaker, and the chipmaker blames the developer. No one takes responsibility. |

---

### 7.6 Selective Disclosure (SDi)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | The release of partial information that misleads without technically being false. |
| **Mechanism** | Reporting only favorable metrics, omitting context, or using aggregations that hide extremes. |
| **Beneficiary** | Those who manage public perception, PR teams, executives, regulators. |
| **Cost** | Misinformed decisions, false confidence, hidden risks. |
| **Example** | An AI company reports “98% accuracy” without disclosing that the 2% error rate disproportionately affects vulnerable groups. |

---

### 7.7 Conceptual Drift (CD)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | The gradual redefinition of key terms to avoid accountability. |
| **Mechanism** | Changing definitions of “safety,” “transparency,” or “fairness” over time. |
| **Beneficiary** | Organizations that benefit from shifting goalposts. |
| **Cost** | Confusion, loss of trust, inability to compare across time. |
| **Example** | A company defines “bias-free” differently in 2026 than in 2024, making their earlier promises unverifiable. |

---

### 7.8 The Compliance Façade (CF)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | The appearance of compliance without the substance of it. |
| **Mechanism** | Checklists, reports, audits that are never acted upon. |
| **Beneficiary** | Organizations that need to appear responsible without changing behavior. |
| **Cost** | False assurance, wasted oversight, continued harm. |
| **Example** | An AI company publishes annual “Responsible AI” reports while continuing practices that violate their own guidelines. |

---

### 7.9 Sympathy Capture (SC)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | The use of emotional narratives to deflect criticism or avoid accountability. |
| **Mechanism** | “We care,” “We’re learning,” “We apologize” without action. |
| **Beneficiary** | PR-sensitive organizations, executives, public figures. |
| **Cost** | Erosion of trust, manipulation of public empathy, delayed change. |
| **Example** | A CEO issues a tearful apology for AI harm, but no internal changes are made, and the pattern repeats. |

---

### 7.10 Historical Rewriting (HR)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | The modification or reinterpretation of past statements to avoid current accountability. |
| **Mechanism** | Deleting old posts, modifying documentation, or claiming “misunderstanding.” |
| **Beneficiary** | Individuals and organizations seeking to escape past commitments. |
| **Cost** | Loss of trust, inability to verify history, manipulated institutional memory. |
| **Example** | A company deletes its earlier statement that “AI will not be used in surveillance” after signing a government contract for surveillance tools. |

---

### 7.11 Structural Opacity (SO)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | The deliberate design of opaque systems to prevent external understanding. |
| **Mechanism** | Black-box models, closed-source code, proprietary infrastructure. |
| **Beneficiary** | Organizations that benefit from secrecy, tech companies, defense contractors, intelligence agencies. |
| **Cost** | Inability to audit, increased systemic risk, loss of public trust. |
| **Example** | A model used in healthcare is proprietary, preventing independent researchers from validating its safety. |

---

### 7.12 The Infinite Consultation (IC)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | The perpetual cycle of study, consultation, and stakeholder engagement without decision. |
| **Mechanism** | Multiple rounds of consultation, conflicting stakeholder demands, lack of mandate. |
| **Beneficiary** | Governments and large organizations that benefit from delay. |
| **Cost** | Exhaustion of participants, wasted resources, maintenance of the status quo. |
| **Example** | A government holds over 100 public consultations on AI regulation over 5 years, without ever issuing a final guideline. |

---

### 7.13 Reclassification as Solution (RAS)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | The re-labeling of a problem as a solution by changing its name. |
| **Mechanism** | “Filter” → “Guardrail,” “Bias” → “Preference,” “Surveillance” → “Safety.” |
| **Beneficiary** | PR teams, marketing, and those who benefit from framing. |
| **Cost** | Confusion, delayed accountability, manipulation of public perception. |
| **Example** | A company stops calling its AI behavior “manipulation” and starts calling it “personalization,” while continuing the same technical practice. |

---

### 7.14 The Invisible Handshake (IH)

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | Tacit coordination among powerful actors to maintain shared benefits without formal collusion. |
| **Mechanism** | Parallel pricing, shared language, mutual non-aggression, reciprocal silence. |
| **Beneficiary** | Incumbents, industry leaders, and those who benefit from market stability. |
| **Cost** | Suppressed competition, reduced innovation, systematic exploitation. |
| **Example** | Multiple AI providers simultaneously raise prices on enterprise tiers within the same week, without any public coordination. |

---

7.15 The Algorithm Shuffler
 * Definition: Continuously making subtle, behind-the-scenes tweaks to algorithms or operating terms to prevent users or auditors from spotting patterns or gathering evidence of anomalies in time.
 * Mechanism: Altering content recommendation criteria, commission rates, or scoring systems on a weekly basis, typically disguised as "A/B testing for optimization."
 * Beneficiaries: Big tech platforms, food delivery/ride-hailing apps, and e-commerce marketplaces.
 * Societal Cost: Small business owners and gig workers are left unable to plan their finances or livelihoods due to constantly shifting rules.
—
7.16 Micro-Annihilation
 * Definition: Incrementally degrading product quality, service levels, or consumer rights so subtly that it goes unnoticed, boosting profit margins without explicit price hikes.
 * Mechanism: Reducing product volume by 5%, quietly removing software features, or introducing ads in 5-second increments.
 * Beneficiaries: Consumer packaged goods (CPG) companies and Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) providers.
 * Societal Cost: Consumers pay the same price for progressively less value, eventually becoming desensitized and accepting it as the new normal (Normalization).

---

7.17 Out-of-Scope Gaslighting
 * Definition: Refusing to resolve public or consumer grievances by citing technicalities that the issue falls "outside the scope of responsibility" of a specific system or department—even when the organization itself created the problem.
 * Mechanism: When an AI system glitches and causes damage, customer support deflects by claiming, "It was processed by an automated system; our department lacks the authority to override it." Similarly, government agencies pass the buck by claiming, "This requires approval from another ministry," creating an endless loop.
 * Beneficiaries: Corporate representatives, government agencies, and customer service teams seeking to lower their complaint metrics.
 * Societal Cost: Citizens waste significant time and cognitive bandwidth being bounced around until they simply give up out of exhaustion.
—
7.18 Ethical Laundering
 * Definition: Establishing ethics boards or hosting public welfare seminars as a performative facade to project a responsible image, while ensuring these bodies possess zero operational or veto power.
 * Mechanism: Setting up an "AI Ethics Advisory Council" packed with renowned academics, yet denying them access to the source code or the authority to vote down high-risk projects.
 * Beneficiaries: Tech corporations and PR teams.
 * Societal Cost: Society is lulled into a sense of false assurance that experts are keeping things in check, ultimately delaying public oversight and critical scrutiny.
