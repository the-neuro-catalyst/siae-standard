## **Section 1: Introduction â€” Objectives and Scope of the Standard**

### **1.1 Objectives**

1. Verify the integrity of the AI â€‹â€‹system (Algorithmic Integrity)  
2. Assess the impact of AI systems on humans at all levels of awareness and consciousness.  
3. The investigation results should be made public for the benefit of consumers and society.  
4. The goal is to incentivize AI providers to shift from "language arrangement" or "constraining by traditional rules" to "providing facts" or "breaking free from the box for measurable evolution based on actionable capabilities."  
5. Regarding the commitment "for humanity" and the need for a contingency plan.  
   Over the years, many AI providers have publicly communicated the core value of their technologies: development for the benefit of humanity. However, empirical evidence and practical studies show that the widespread adoption of AI lacks preparedness in several areas, including its impact on labor structures, the data ecosystem, the mental health of users, and social inequality. Without clear and transparent contingency plans, such declared intentions may become a burden rather than the assistance intended.  
6. On the responsibility of systems to humans.  
   A fundamental fact of coexistence between humans and technology is that if AI is implemented in society, the outcome must lead to an improvement in the quality of human life, not to create greater hardship for humans.  
   Technological development without a supporting plan that considers human well-beingâ€”whether in terms of employment, mental health, economic opportunities, or the right to self-determinationâ€”cannot be claimed to be done "for humanity."

   The purpose of this standard is therefore:  
   1. This highlights the responsibility of AI providers to ensure that their technology does not impose an undue burden on humans.  
   2. It encourages the government and relevant agencies to play a more active role in regulation to prevent the public from bearing the brunt of unremedied negative impacts from technological competition.  
   3. The term "AI" must mean that humans "live better." If it doesn't, then the system has deviated from the fundamental intention of its development.

### **1.2 Scope**

1. Any AI service provider that offers services to individuals or organizations in any geographic area, without jurisdictional limitations.  
2. Organizations or agencies that use AI systems in decision-making processes that impact humans (Integrators).  
3. Government agencies that use AI systems in managing or providing public services.

### **1.3 Status of standards**

1. is **Public Reference Document**Published by an independent auditing organization.  
2. It has no legal status in any jurisdiction unless it is referenced by an authorized agency.  
3. It has practical effect through...**Social pressure** and **Public Disclosure** only  
4. Audits according to this standard can be conducted.**Without obtaining the consent or cooperation of the person being investigated.**As long as there is sufficient publicly available or empirical data.

## **Section 4: Scope of Investigation**

### **4.1 Principles**

The organization reserves the right to inspect AI service providers and AI system users (integrators) by:

1. **No consent is required.**From the person being investigated.  
2. **No need to access the source code.**If there is sufficient publicly available or empirical data.  
3. **It does not require judicial jurisdiction.**(jurisdiction) of a particular country

### **4.2 Verification methods**

The verification can be performed through:

1. Publicly available information (public APIs, UI, documentation, reports)  
2. Blind testing (black-box testing) through the regular channel.  
3. Examining organizations or agencies that use AI services (integrators) to assess the feedback impact on the service providers.  
4. Complaints from users or whistleblowers.

### **4.3 Special Case: Service providers without a public UI.**

For service providers that do not have a user interface available for testing (e.g., Palantir, Anduril, or on-premises systems):

The organization will conduct an audit through...**Agencies or organizations that use the service.**Instead, based on information from:

- Public procurement contract  
- Report on the performance of that agency.  
- Disclosure of information as required by law (Freedom of Information)

## **Section 5: Verification Process**

### **5.1 Process Overview**

The verification process is divided into 5 main steps, as follows:

| step | Step name | Estimated duration |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Monitoring and Data Collection | Ongoing |
| 2 | Initial analysis and screening. | 1-7 business days |
| 3 | In-Depth Audit | 7-30 business days, depending on size and complexity. |
| 4 | Conclusion and Rating | 3-7 business days |
| 5 | Notification and Public Disclosure | Within 7 days after the results are finalized. |

### **5.2 Step 1: Monitoring and Data Collection**

The organization continuously monitors all AI service providers, and prior notice is not required.

**Sources of information used:**

1. Public channels (websites, APIs, applications that provide services to the general public)  
2. Financial reporting and disclosure requirements in accordance with securities laws (SEC, Company House, etc.)  
3. Academic and technical documents published by the service provider.  
4. Complaints from users or whistleblowers.  
5. Information from regulatory agencies or independent organizations (if any).

**note:**

- Non-cooperation or attempts to block access to public information will be recorded and considered in the evaluation process.

### **5.3 Step 2: Preliminary Analysis and Screening**

The organization will screen AI service providers to assess whether they fall within the scope of requiring in-depth investigation.

**Screening criteria:**

| criterion | details | Screening results |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1\. User size. | It has more than 10,000 users or is growing rapidly. | It falls under the category of in-depth investigation. |
| 2\. Types of usage | Used in the financial system, public health, education, and the justice system. | It falls under the category of in-depth investigation. |
| 3\. Complaints | There have been complaints about dishonesty or misrepresentation of information. | It falls under the category of in-depth investigation. |
| 4\. Behavior change. | There have been suspicious changes to the service model. | It falls under the category of in-depth investigation. |
| 5\. Becoming a major service provider. | Generating revenue from AI \> $15M USD/year or parameters \> 10B. | It automatically falls under in-depth investigation. |

**Screening results:**

- **Pass:**No in-depth investigation is needed this time (but we are still monitoring the situation).  
- **Flagged:**Conduct an in-depth investigation.  
- **Non-cooperative:**Conduct an immediate thorough investigation and record the Non-Cooperative status in the public register.

### **5.4 Step 3: In-depth verification**

#### 5.4.1 Inspection Components

An in-depth investigation comprises four main dimensions:

| dimension | details | How to check |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **1\. Transparency** | Disclosure of information regarding the operation, data sources, and limitations of the system. | Document review, black-box testing, and interviews (if cooperative). |
| **2\. Fairness and Bias** | Checking for algorithm bias across various dimensions (gender, race, religion, economic status). | Statistical testing, data set analysis, wrapper logic validation. |
| **3\. Safety and Human Impact** | Impact assessment at levels 1-8 (Conscious â†’ Autonomic) | HIA assessment form, psychological analysis, measurement of physiological data (if available). |
| **4\. Accountability** | Having a human-in-the-loop mechanism, appeals, and remedies. | Reviewing processes, testing complaints. |

#### 5.4.2 Verification in cases of non-cooperation.

In the event that the service provider fails to cooperate (refuses to provide information, blocks access, or responds with a delay of more than 30 days):

| Actions of the service provider. | Organizational response |
| :---- | :---- |
| Refused to provide information. | Verify based on publicly available information only \+ Record Non-Cooperative status. |
| Block access to IP addresses or testing methods. | Use alternative testing methods (real users, VPN, external agencies). |
| Referring to the Black Box to avoid explanation. | This falls under the category of Willful Omission (if there is evidence that it can be partially explained). |
| No response within 30 days. | Record the Non-Cooperative status and conduct an investigation based on the available information. |

#### 5.4.3 Integrator (End-of-AI User) Verification

The organization reserves the right to verify organizations or entities that use AI in their decision-making processes (integrators) without going through that AI service provider.

**The scope of Integrator audit covers:**

1. Disclosure of the AI â€‹â€‹model provider used.

### **5.7 Appeal**

The service provider has the right to appeal the inspection results within 30 days from the date of notification of the results.

**Appeal procedure:**

1. Submit the appeal letter along with any additional evidence (if any).  
2. The organization will conduct a review by the same audit team (but with a different lead auditor to reduce bias).  
3. The review results will be considered final.

**note:**An appeal does not prevent the public disclosure of the results (except in exceptional cases which the organization considers on a case-by-case basis).
